## Sam Harris - Free Will

A long while ago I read a book by Sam Harris - a very outspoken atheist who is supposedly on a mission to establish a set of morals using purely science. He openly dislikes religion in general, but especially Christianity (I believe). For some reason this makes me curious to hear what he has to say, so I started listening to some of his podcasts and then a book of his perked by interest. It was called ‘Free Will’.

It was a rather unsettling book to read, which left me with a week long existential crisis after having done so. Its a pretty short book so I invite you to read it, so that you can really understand what Sam Harris is purporting. If you don’t have time, though, that’s ok, I will give you a quote from Sam Harris that does a good job of summarizing:

“You are not in control of your own mind, because you, as a conscious agent, are only part of your mind, living at the mercy of the other parts”

What I believe Sam Harris is saying here is that the part of your brain that experiences consciousness, the part that you are aware of, is closer to the analogy of a man watching a TV, then an operator of a vehicle. The parts of the brain that actually makes decisions, the other parts, simply make ‘you’ (you being the conscious part of your brain) aware of the actions that they decided to take, immediately after they decide it.

Essentially you simply perceive the actions as being decisions of your own making, when in reality the ‘you’ is really just watching them happen in real time. This is less so a logical argument, more of an observation about how the human mind works. I again invite you to read the book (more of a short essay) to understand Sam Harris’ perspective more clearly.

## Alex O’Connor - the source

If first heard this idea in a podcast by Alex O’Conner called ‘Within Reason’. Although it wasn’t the main point of the conversation, most of which went over my head, this is something that resonated with me.

If you think about any action you take and ask why you did that, you can categorize all the possible answers as having either an internal or an external motive. Hear i use motive to mean the main reason behind the decision being made - in effect the actual making of the decision. With each motive we can analyze whether it was internal or external and therefore determine if we can actually claim responsibility for the decision.

If the action was taken because of something internal we can try to identify that reason at the highest level - ex. ‘I ate the chocolate because I like chocolate’. The same categorization can be done again as to why the highest level supporting reason for the action holds true - ‘Why do I like the chocolate? Well because it tastes good on my tongue. (an internal motivation again).’

This elicitation of the supporting reason of every successive motive can be continued until either:

A. The lowest level and therefore primary motivation is determined to be something external

B. The lowest possible level of motivation is reached

In either cases the root motive for any action can be seen to be entirely outside of our control. In the first case, A, of an external force being the root motivation, the external force is obviously outside of our control and therefore does not support free will.

The second case requires figuring out what the lowest possible layer is, which I purport to be the most fundamental laws of physics of the universe. These are determined by God and outside of our control and therefore this is actually case A.

One may try and argue that the unique qualities that make an individual who they are could be the lowest possible level. With this, we could start fairly high level by saying the person was angry → they are angry because they in a heated argument and the person is given to anger → they are given to anger because of either the environment they grew up in (external) or their biology and psychology (supposedly internal) which is based on their DNA. At this point, we exit the levels of motive which could be defined as being qualities of the human and enter the very lowest levels that make up the world itself. At this point it is no longer possible to pin the motivation on the person, because the ‘motives’ are now more like the interactions of the most basic units of the universe itself. Their DNA is based on biology → chemistry → physics → observations of the behaviour of the universe.

Quite obviously the individual has no control over the interactions between the individual cells that make them up, the molecules the cells are made of, the protons, neutrons, and electrons constructing the atoms, the quarks making up the subatomic particles, and the strings (supposedly) making up the quarks.

Who knows what lies beyond the strings - the point is, at this point we have exited the realm of the human mind and are therefor outside of its control. In the end, we see that all actions are based on external motivations and therefore outside of the control of the individual and therefore we have no control, never actually make a decision, and therefore have no free will.

## The Oracle

If you have ever watched The Matrix, then you may be familiar with the scene where Neo (the main character) is sitting on a park bench in an unknown location with a woman called the Oracle. If you have not, you don’t need much context to understand the importance of the interaction. Basically just that the Oracle is a character who possesses the power of foresight.

Neo and the Oracle are having a conversation concerning the plot of the movie when the Oracle (O) offers Neo (N) a candy, it goes like this:

- O: “Candy?”
- N: “Do you already know if I’m going to take it?”
- O: “Wouldn’t be much of an oracle if I didn’t.”
- N: “But if you already know, how can I make a choice?”
- O: “Because you didn’t come here to make a choice. You’ve already made it. You’re here try to understand why you made it.”

Technically, only the first 4 lines are directly relevant to this point, but I included the 5th one because I found it incredibly profound, interesting, and worth some thought.

The main point I raise by bringing up this conversation is found in Neo’s second line where he asks that if the Oracle already knows what choice Neo is going to make - how can he actually be making a choice at all? The reasoning behind this is that someone or something has already made the decision for him, and he is merely here to act that decision out.

The parallels between our world and the artificially simulated Matrix are quite straightforward:

- God’s omniscience is represented by the Oracle
- We humans are represented by Neo
- The choice of accepting or denying the candy represents all our actions
- The ability to make a choice represents our free will

God, is in fact, omniscient. God knows all, including all past, present, and future events. This is because God is the creator of these events. I don’t intend to argue this point, I will assume this as truth.

The logic is simple. At some point, God determined that on April 12, 2026 @ 10:32 PM, at my home in Orangeville, as I sit here on my couch typing on my laptop, I would write this sentence. The decision was already made for me that I would be here contemplating this matter and writing out my thoughts. How could it be, that if someone determined for me every move that I would make from day 1 till the day I die, that I have free will? I never have the capacity to make decisions because those decisions have already been laid out for me.

Think of children for example. Up until sometime in their teens, children have little choice over the, especially important, matters of their lives. If the parent decides the child will go to this school or that, how could we say the child decided where it shall go to school.

Likewise, how can we say we have ever made a decision if God has already decided for us?

## Determinism

Perhaps you have already heard of determinism used in the context of free will. Google defines it as: Determinism - Relating to the philosophical doctrine that all events, including human action, are ultimately determined by causes regarded as external to the will. This is not what I intended of this argument, but you could say it is the ultimate destination of it

I am thinking of determinism in the context of math, Google again defines: Determinism - involves systems or models that produce the same, unique output from a given starting condition or input, with no randomness involved. The most important parts of this definition is: system … starting condition … no randomness

You may have heard before a famous quote from Albert Einstein “God does not play dice”. Albert Einstein was known for this quote, which was based on his rejection of quantum randomness, which is defined (again by Google) as: Quantum Randomness - the inherent, fundamental unpredictability of physical events at the subatomic level, where outcomes are not predetermined by prior information.

Albert Einstein rejected this teaching, alleging that the world was in fact deterministic (God does not play dice → there is no randomness). An amazing quality about deterministic systems is that they can be modeled, always.

This means that given the correct model, and the correct inputs, the outputs for any system can be calculated. For any system which is temporal, its state at different times can also be accurately calculated at any point in time. This means that for any temporal system, we could theoretically map out every state at every point in time, for the entire existence of that system.

Albert Einstein believed that the world is deterministic. If that is true that means, that given a large enough model (which I cannot even imagine how complex that would be), and the correct starting points of the universe, we could in fact calculate every single action that would ever be taken by any human ever for the entire course of history.

The argument then lies on whether or not one believes the world is truly deterministic. I would argue that it is by denying the opposite. If the world is non-deterministic, then there is a measure of randomness to it. If this is true, then there is a measure of action within the world that is outside of God’s control. I don’t believe this to be true as God is omniscient and omnipotent, and therefore something of this world could not be outside of his control.
