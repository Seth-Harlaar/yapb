## Christ’s death on the cross

Typically, when someone is making a logical argument, they begin with the premises that act as the building blocks of the argument, they then construct the argument using logical patterns and reach a conclusion.

One of the ways I like to analyze an argument is to continue this process and examine the implications of the conclusion. This can be done by assuming the arrived upon conclusion to be true and then exploring the space of realities that this truth would create if it was true.

We can do this with free will to see that it in fact must be real.

We can start by assuming that we do not have free will and we can explore the implications of this. If we do not have free will, then we effectively have no responsibility for any of our actions. If we do not have any responsibility for our actions then we have technically never committed any sins. If we have committed any sins then we are not in need of being saved from said sins. This is an implication that we have reached that absolutely cannot be true, given the many verses of the Bible that indicate that we are in fact sinful beings and in dire need of salvation.

A step farther reveals that if we are not in need of saving, then Christ’s death on the cross would have been entirely pointless and an exercise in waste.

Since we know these two things to be false, we can say that free will must therefore be true.

## Canons of Dort

While free will is not directly addressed in any of our confessions (to my knowledge), it is implied as being real in the Canons of Dort.

Article 1 in the section titled ‘The Third and Fourth Main Points of Doctrine’ states ‘However, rebelling against God at the devil’s instigation and by their own free will, they deprived themselves of these outstanding gifts.’ (taken from https://www.crcna.org/welcome/beliefs/confessions/canons-dort)

The sentence states that man, with his own free will, rebelled against God. This assumes that free will is real, and that man can in fact be responsible for his actions.
